## Title: Harnessing interdisciplinarity in the investigation of nucleotide sequence evolution
### Abstract: 

Interdisciplinarity is an environmental state as well as an individual state. It refers to either a state of collaboration among individuals representing traditional academic fields or to a state in which an individual is proficient in how multiple fields think, communicate, and operate. In this work, interdisciplinarity is explored at the intersection of three academic fields: bioinformatics, chemistry, and HCI (Human-Computer Interaction). 

First, physicochemical property information is incorporated into codon substitution models at site-level resolution to provide a mechanistic understanding of how natural selection is constrained by such properties. Three PRIME (PRoperty-Informed Models of Evolution) models estimate the effects of property constraints--hydrophobicity, volume, electrical charge, alpha-helix propensity and beta-sheet propensity--at varying scopes. G-PRIME estimates the global effects of these five constraints across a sequence alignment, while E-PRIME captures episodic effects by estimating heterogeneity ($K=2$) within an alignment and then inferring property constraints for each class. S-PRIME estimates property constraints at each site in an alignment. The PREI (PRIME Imputation) method infers probabilities for 61 sense codon states at each site in an alignment based on S-PRIME, providing insights into how a site may evolve given evolutionary history and physicochemical constraints. PRIME models are compared with state-of-the-art methods such as CoRa (Conservation/Radical) and ESM-2 (Evolutionary Scale Modeling) using simulated and empirical datasets. 

Second, scientific communication for bioinformatics methods--specifically HyPhy (Hypothesis testing using Phylogenies), which models nucleotide sequence evolution--is investigated using concepts from HCI. The affordances of both traditional and GenAI (generative artificial intelligence) e-tutorials are examined across three studies. In Study 1, traditional e-tutorials from 22 representative bioinformatics software tools were collected and analyzed using quantitative and qualitative content analysis. These e-tutorials are not merely step-by-step guides: they are well-grounded, particularly in computer science, and serve as key resources for communication and collaboration. In Study 2, the potential of GenAI e-tutorials was explored through an autoethnographic approach. The autoethnographer identified how information-seeking behaviors with GenAI can be described using the conceptual frameworks of orienteering and information scent. In Study 3, information-seeking behaviors using traditional and retrieval-augmented GenAI (RAG) e-tutorials were compared under the orienteering framework. Data were collected from 10 participants through lab studies, surveys, and interviews. The exploring search state was observed only in the RAG e-tutorial group, likely due to an increased sense of latitude.

Harnessing interdisciplinarity pushes the boundaries of scientific inquiry. The contributions of the work are methodological, empirical, artifact-based, and opinion-based. The holistic value of interdisciplinary research is evident, underscoring the further need for collaboration across fields and for improving such collaboration.
